Chapter 5

Federalists: The Musical, Case Two: rapture insurance.

The second staged case asks whether the state can enforce a contract whose trigger depends on transcendence without becoming an administrator of metaphysical truth.

In the second thought experiment, a Jewish resident of New York sells "rapture insurance" to evangelical Christians and is sued after a policyholder is allegedly taken up in the rapture. The dispute appears comic at first glance, but its constitutional stakes are exacting. If a court enforces the contract as written, it must effectively determine whether a salvific event has occurred. If it rejects the claim on theological grounds, it still places itself in the role of metaphysical interpreter.

This case sharpens the dissertation's argument because it joins contract, evidence, belief, and eschatology. The state is competent to assess fraud, reliance, damages, and public representation in worldly terms. It is not competent to certify transcendence itself. The staged form reveals how easily ordinary legal categories can tempt the state into theological administration when the underlying dispute depends on revelation rather than public fact.

Metaphysical promise and civil adjudication

The chapter argues that contracts tied to unverifiable salvation events expose a boundary in civil judgment. Courts may evaluate whether parties misrepresented terms or exploited belief, but they cannot pronounce on whether the promised metaphysical condition has been satisfied. Doing so would convert transcendence into a justiciable administrative category.

Why comedy is methodologically useful

The case draws on theatrical absurdity to make jurisdictional overreach newly visible. Humor here is not a retreat from seriousness. It is a Brechtian device that prevents pious naturalization and forces the audience to watch the legal machinery at work. The strange case makes a familiar danger harder to miss.

Resulting claim

A constitutionally restrained state can resolve the civil dimensions of a metaphysical contract only by refusing to certify the metaphysical event itself. The refusal is not evasion. It is evidence that the state remains bounded by the limits of public judgment.
