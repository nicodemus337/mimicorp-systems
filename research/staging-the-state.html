Research Project

Staging the State

Prospectus: constitutional argument, public persuasion, and narrative form in the founding-era print sphere.

This prospectus outlines a study of how competing constitutional visions were staged for public judgment in the late eighteenth century, with particular attention to how argument, voice, and rhetorical framing operate across the Federalist Papers and selected Anti-Federalist texts as a contested field of political narration.

In dissertation form, the project asks how constitutional discourse made institutions imaginable, emotionally legible, and economically persuasive for a reading public that was being asked not simply to interpret a new state, but to inhabit it.

Project Rationale

Why this project matters now.

Scholarship on the founding period has long treated constitutional debate as a problem of doctrine, institutional design, or political theory. This project argues that such debate also depended on narrative form: scenes of danger, figures of authority, staged audiences, and recurring plots through which constitutional order became thinkable.

The project therefore intervenes at the intersection of political theory, rhetoric, and literary-historical analysis. It asks not only what competing authors believed the state should be, but how they scripted consent, fear, legitimacy, and economic order for public uptake.

Research Question

How was “the state” made legible as a narrative object rather than only a legal one?

The central question asks how constitutional authority was narrated, dramatized, and distributed through public writing. Rather than treating founding-era texts as static containers of doctrine, the project reads them as staged interventions that position readers, anticipate conflict, and model competing understandings of political order.

Theoretical Framework

Politics as performance, political economy as enactment.

This project builds on Eric Gorham's concept of the "Theater of Politics", which treats political life as inherently performative, structured through roles, scripts, and staged interaction.

While Gorham focuses on communication and rhetorical performance, this project extends that framework into political economy by analyzing how competing economic logics are not only argued but enacted through structured roles and institutional design.

The broader claim is that constitutional discourse does not merely describe political order; it stages relations among institutions, publics, and economic expectations in a form that invites identification, resistance, and judgment.

Data + Approach

Primary corpus and analytical frame.

The core corpus consists of the Federalist Papers, a selected body of Anti-Federalist texts including the Brutus letters and Centinel I, and related constitutional commentary used to establish rhetorical context. The project combines close reading with computational analysis in order to trace patterns of diction, address, thematic clustering, and argumentative sequencing across competing constitutional publics.

In prospectus terms, the project proceeds from a bounded textual corpus toward a mixed interpretive method: first establishing a comparative archive, then testing how rhetorical performance, institutional imagination, and economic language cohere across opposed constitutional positions.

The corpus will expand in later stages to include constitutional convention debates, selected pamphlet literature, and editorial framing where such materials clarify how these texts circulated and were received within a wider argumentative environment.

Method

Computational comparison paired with narrative modeling.

Methodologically, the project combines corpus preparation, token-level comparison, thematic grouping, and rhetorical pattern detection with narrative modeling attentive to scene, speaker position, implied audience, and temporal framing. Computational methods help surface recurrent structures and lexical contrasts, while narrative analysis asks how those structures organize political imagination, legitimacy, and fear.

The working hypothesis is that debates over constitutional order are inseparable from performed economic assumptions: scale, representation, consolidation, credit, and administrative authority appear not simply as concepts but as enacted relations among speakers, institutions, and publics.

In prospectus form, the method is meant to show both evidentiary procedure and interpretive stakes: computational comparison establishes recurring formal patterns across the archive, while narrative modeling clarifies how those patterns stage claims about governance, authority, and economic order.

Contribution

A contribution to political theory, rhetorical studies, and early American analysis.

The project contributes a reading of founding-era constitutional debate as a field of performed political economy rather than a purely abstract dispute over institutional form. By treating constitutional argument as staged interaction, it reframes familiar texts as narrative technologies of state formation.

It also contributes methodologically by placing computational comparison in service of interpretive argument rather than substituting quantification for reading. The intended outcome is a dissertation-scale study that remains legible to publication venues in political theory, rhetorical criticism, and interdisciplinary humanities research.

Dissertation Path

Prospective chapter structure.

The dissertation is presently imagined as a multi-chapter study moving from theoretical framing into corpus construction, comparative analysis of Federalist and Anti-Federalist staging, and a concluding account of constitutional discourse as a performed political economy.

  • Chapter 1: theoretical framework and historiographic intervention.
  • Chapter 2: corpus, archive, and method.
  • Chapter 3: Federalist scenes of legitimacy, scale, and authority.
  • Chapter 4: Anti-Federalist scenes of consolidation, fear, and refusal.
  • Chapter 5: political economy, narration, and the staging of institutional life.

Publication Path

Article and public scholarship trajectory.

In publication terms, the project is designed to yield both dissertation chapters and article-length interventions. Individual segments could be developed into work on founding-era performance, constitutional narration, or the relation between rhetorical form and political economy.

The project is also suited to a parallel public-facing track in which selected findings are translated into essayistic or audio form without collapsing the scholarly argument into general-interest summary.
