Research Project

Staging the State

Prospectus: constitutional discourse, public judgment, and the limits of state power in spiritual matters.

Prospectus Overview

Staging the State examines how constitutional authority is made legible not only through doctrine and institutional design, but through performance, narrative form, and public judgment. Beginning with the Federalist Papers, selected Anti-Federalist texts, and the founding-era print sphere, the project asks how the American state was imagined, voiced, and staged for a public being asked to inhabit a new political order. It argues that constitutional discourse did not merely describe institutions. It rendered them credible, affectively intelligible, and narratively inhabitable. This website is therefore not a companion, prospecting shell, or public-facing supplement to a dissertation elsewhere. It is the dissertation's native scholarly form: the principal scene in which the argument appears, is sequenced, and is made available for judgment.

The dissertation extends that founding archive into a broader theoretical inquiry: what are the limits of the state regarding the spirit of its people? More specifically, it treats separation of church and state not simply as an institutional arrangement or a narrow constitutional doctrine, but as a principle of political humility and jurisdictional restraint. A constitutional state may regulate conduct, adjudicate disputes, and maintain public order, but it cannot safely govern the soul, certify transcendence, define sacrilege as theological fact, or fully administer spiritual injury without exceeding its rightful bounds. That claim is advanced here through a staged scholarly environment whose architecture, modularity, and navigational logic are part of the method by which constitutional legibility is studied.

To develop that argument, the project places founding-era constitutional thought in conversation with Hannah Arendt, Eric Gorham, and Bertolt Brecht, while using an original musical framework, Federalists: The Musical, as a method for staging constitutional conflict. In this framework, the audience assumes the role of the Supreme Court and is visited by arguments drawn from American political thought as it confronts two fictional but theoretically revealing cases. In the first, a Louisiana man is sued after opening a drive-through daiquiri shop called "Mohammad's Drive Through Daiquiris," featuring an artistic depiction of the Prophet Muhammad as its logo. In the second, a Jewish resident of New York is sued after offering "rapture insurance" to evangelical Christians when a policyholder is allegedly "raptured" under the terms of the policy. These cases serve as paired constitutional thought experiments. The first asks whether the state can restrain expression on the basis of sacred offense without becoming an arbiter of sacrilege. The second asks whether the state can enforce metaphysical promise and certify eschatological events without becoming an administrator of transcendence.

Through these staged disputes, the dissertation argues that the Supreme Court must be understood not only as a legal institution, but as a theatrical apparatus of political world-making in a plural republic. Constitutional judgment is shown to be inseparable from performance, symbolic mediation, and the management of public meaning. Drawing on Arendt's account of politics and plurality, Gorham's performative model of political life, Brecht's theory of estrangement and theatrical critique, and the Federalist tradition's concern with faction, scale, and institutional mediation, the project shows that the state's legitimacy depends in part on its refusal to claim authority over domains it cannot rightly master. The website's own structure extends that claim: it presents the dissertation as a series of linked scenes rather than a single linear container, treating public legibility as a formal problem of arrangement as well as interpretation.

At its core, Staging the State argues that the American constitutional tradition is haunted by a recurring problem: the state needs enough authority to govern a plural people, but not so much authority that it presumes jurisdiction over the deepest sources of that people's meaning. By reading constitutional argument as staged action rather than doctrine alone, the dissertation contributes to political theory, constitutional thought, rhetorical studies, and performance theory while offering a new way to think about the relationship between law, public judgment, and the spiritual limits of state power. Its publication infrastructure is part of that claim. The project is self-published through Linode as a deliberate scholarly choice, with self-managed infrastructure supplying the material conditions of circulation, access, sequencing, and persistence.

Project Rationale

Why this project matters now.

Scholarship on the founding period has often treated constitutional debate as a problem of doctrine, institutional design, or political theory. That remains essential, but it is not sufficient. Constitutional order also had to be rendered as a scene: danger had to be paced, authority had to be voiced, readers had to be positioned as judges, and the state had to be made imaginable before it could be obeyed.

I preserve that emphasis on staging, voice, rhetorical framing, and public judgment, but I now place it in a larger theoretical arc. The deeper issue is not only how the state became legible as a narrative object, but what that legibility was for and where its jurisdiction ends. Founding-era constitutional discourse provides an archive of mediation, faction, persuasion, and institutional design; it also furnishes the conceptual conditions for a later argument about the boundaries of civil power in matters of spiritual injury, sacred offense, and metaphysical claim-making.

The dissertation therefore moves from the ratification-era print sphere into a broader account of constitutional discourse as performed argument. It studies how public persuasion makes institutions imaginable, and it asks what happens when institutions are asked to decide questions they cannot decide without becoming something other than civil authorities. Because the project is about staging, mediation, sequencing, and public judgment, it is published in the medium most adequate to those concerns. The site is the dissertation's principal scene of appearance, and its native web form is a methodological commitment rather than a delivery convenience.

Research Question

What are the limits of the state regarding the spirit of its people?

The central question of the dissertation is how far a constitutional state can go into the spiritual domain before it ceases to act as a civil authority and becomes instead an arbiter of sacrilege, transcendence, or salvation. I argue that church-state separation is best understood here as a doctrine of restraint: the state may regulate conduct and adjudicate worldly disputes, but it cannot legitimately certify sacred meaning or administer the inward life without overreaching its jurisdiction.

A linked sub-question remains crucial to the project's historical and rhetorical method: how is the state staged, narrated, and made publicly legible as an object of judgment rather than merely described as a legal structure? The dissertation therefore treats constitutional discourse as a performed form of argument that not only advances claims but also arranges scenes of public judgment through which those claims become credible.

  • How do Federalist and Anti-Federalist writers construct scenes of danger, scale, authority, and trust for readers asked to inhabit an emerging constitutional order?
  • How do pseudonymity, serial publication, and print circulation shape constitutional voice and the formation of a public capable of judgment?
  • How does the founding archive establish later American arguments about jurisdiction, conscience, offense, and the distinction between civil order and spiritual life?
  • How can staged constitutional thought experiments clarify the point at which adjudication of sacred offense or metaphysical promise exceeds the lawful competence of the state?

Literature Review

From ratification and print circulation to law, performance, and spiritual jurisdiction.

Foundational work on the Revolution and founding-era political thought by Bernard Bailyn, Gordon S. Wood, and Jack N. Rakove establishes the constitutional and intellectual stakes of the ratification era, while scholarship by Saul Cornell, Joyce Appleby, and John Phillip Reid clarifies the relation between dissent, representation, republicanism, and political economy. I build on that scholarship while shifting emphasis from doctrine alone to the forms through which doctrine became publicly legible and politically durable.

Work on Anti-Federalist thought, especially Herbert J. Storing's account of Anti-Federalist political thought, has shown that opposition to the Constitution cannot be reduced to nostalgia or mere obstruction. This project extends that insight by reading Federalist and Anti-Federalist writing as rival modes of staging scale, legitimacy, fear, and institutional trust. Their arguments were not only doctrinally opposed; they were also formally distinct ways of placing the reader inside scenes of judgment about what kind of authority could be trusted.

A second body of scholarship on print culture and publicity, especially Michael Warner's work on publication and the public sphere, alongside debates about ideological language and circulation, makes clear that eighteenth-century political argument operated through seriality, mediated address, and readership formation. I retain that framework because it explains how constitutional discourse made institutions imaginable before they were stable. Yet I also argue that these scenes of public formation matter because they prepare later conflicts over the state's jurisdiction: they teach us how a constitutional order authorizes judgment while also revealing what judgment cannot rightfully settle.

The project therefore joins scholarship on rhetoric and the public sphere with work on political performance and law's symbolic form. In conversation with Nancy Fraser, Michael Warner, and Eric B. Gorham's performative model of politics, I treat constitutional discourse as staged argument rather than inert doctrine. That move opens onto a broader theoretical literature on the limits of politics, public judgment, and the distinction between worldly and inward life, especially in Hannah Arendt, as well as performance theory that can account for legal and constitutional conflict as theatrical arrangement rather than merely textual dispute.

Church-state separation enters the project at this juncture not as an isolated field of First Amendment doctrine, but as the place where the problem of political limits becomes clearest. Questions of blasphemy, offense, sacrilege, revelation, and eschatological promise reveal what civil authority may regulate and what it cannot know, certify, or repair on theological terms. The founding archive supplies the vocabulary of mediation, faction, and institutional design; the later constitutional thought experiments test that vocabulary under pressure.

Theoretical Framework

Arendt, Gorham, Brecht, and American political thought as distinct analytic tools.

Hannah Arendt provides the dissertation's clearest account of political limits. Her distinctions among plurality, judgment, worldliness, and inward life help specify why institutions cannot safely govern every domain they touch. Arendt's importance here is not that she offers a ready-made theory of church-state separation, but that she helps explain why politics is bound to a shared public world and why state authority becomes dangerous when it reaches beyond that world into conscience, transcendence, or the administration of the soul.

Eric Gorham remains central to the project. His account of the Theater of Politics clarifies how political and legal institutions operate through roles, scripts, audiences, and symbolic staging. I strengthen Gorham's role by using his work to read constitutional adjudication, and especially the Supreme Court, as a site of public theatrical judgment. The Court does not merely apply doctrine; it performs the state's claim to lawful competence before an audience that must decide whether such performance is persuasive or excessive.

Bertolt Brecht justifies the project's musical and theatrical method. Brechtian estrangement, anti-illusionism, and exposure of social machinery provide a way to stage constitutional conflict without reducing it to immersive sentiment or private identification. The point of the musical form is not embellishment. It is to make the operations of judgment, jurisdiction, and legal narration visible, audible, and open to critical scrutiny. Brecht's influence helps prevent the audience from becoming passive spectators to doctrine; they are instead positioned as judges who must confront the state's limits.

American political thought beginning with the Federalist Papers supplies the historical foundation and argumentative archive. Madison, Hamilton, the Anti-Federalists, and the wider print sphere of ratification furnish the dissertation's initial account of faction, mediation, institutional design, and public persuasion. These texts are not merely background. They establish the constitutional forms of argument through which later disputes about sacred offense, conscience, metaphysical promise, and jurisdiction can be staged.

Analytic Extension

Federalists: The Musical as constitutional thought experiment.

The dissertation's key analytic extension is Federalists: The Musical, which functions not as a side project or whimsical appendage, but as a rigorous theoretical device for staging constitutional conflict. The musical asks what the state can judge without ceasing to be a civil authority, and it does so by turning legal doctrine into audible, contestable, public argument.

The musical is structured around two fictional Supreme Court cases. In the first, a Louisiana man is sued by an Islamic church after opening a drive-through daiquiri shop in Lake Charles called Mohammad's Drive Through Daiquiris and using an artistic depiction of the Prophet Muhammad as its logo. The case tests whether the state can restrain expression because of sacred offense without becoming an arbiter of sacrilege. In the second, a Jewish resident of New York is sued after offering "rapture insurance" to evangelical Christians; a Christian policyholder was allegedly raptured as defined in the policy, and the plaintiffs seek damages for non-payment. That case tests whether the state can enforce metaphysical promise and certify eschatological events without becoming an administrator of transcendence.

Together, these cases ask how far a constitutional state can go into the spiritual domain before it exceeds its rightful jurisdiction. They sharpen the dissertation's governing claim: the state may regulate public conduct and adjudicate civil injury, but it cannot define theological truth, certify sacrilege as sacrilege, confirm salvation, or decide whether transcendence has taken place.

The audience in the musical plays the role of the Supreme Court and is visited by arguments drawn from rival traditions within American political thought. The pairings are illustrative rather than exhaustive: Case 1 stages James Madison against John C. Calhoun, while Case 2 stages Alexander Hamilton against William James. These pairings clarify that constitutional judgment is dramatized through contesting traditions of authority, pluralism, sovereignty, pragmatism, and spiritual claim-making rather than through a single doctrinal voice.

Archive + Approach

Primary archive, historical grounding, and interpretive scale.

The core historical archive consists of the Federalist Papers, a selected body of Anti-Federalist texts including the Brutus letters and Centinel I, and related constitutional commentary that clarifies publication, circulation, and reception. I read these writings as staged arguments in which institutions become imaginable through voice, seriality, and public address.

The archive expands selectively into later materials on church-state separation, legal performance, and religious injury where those materials help trace how the founding vocabulary of authority and restraint develops into later disputes over the state's competence in spiritual matters. Expansion serves the central argument rather than becoming a separate archive-building exercise.

The project remains interdisciplinary across political theory, rhetoric, literary history, and performance studies, but it is disciplined by a single problem: how constitutional discourse stages legitimate civil power while also revealing the point at which that power should stop.

Method

Narrative analysis and performance theory, with computational work in a supporting role.

Methodologically, the dissertation centers on close reading, rhetorical analysis, narrative modeling, and performance analysis. I track scene, speaker position, implied audience, temporal framing, and the distribution of judgment across texts and staged situations. The main interpretive task is to show how constitutional arguments are performed: how they position a public, what they ask that public to feel, and what they presume the state can know or decide.

The dissertation's digital form is also methodological. The website is not a neutral vessel for completed prose, but a staged scholarly environment in which navigation, modularity, sequencing, and public legibility are themselves part of the argument. Because the project studies how constitutional authority becomes imaginable through arrangement and address, its own architecture participates in that inquiry. Pages, links, sectional transitions, and the relation between overview and extension all help model the problem of constitutional appearance.

Computational comparison remains available, but only in a subordinate and clarifying capacity. Where lexical clustering or pattern detection can illuminate recurrent contrasts around union, consolidation, conscience, injury, or authority, I will use those tools to support interpretive claims already anchored in the archive. The project does not depend on computation for its conceptual coherence, and it does not treat corpus design as its center of gravity.

The musical framework introduces a second methodological register. As a Brechtian device, it permits constitutional argument to be tested in performance rather than merely described in prose. The audience's role as Supreme Court becomes part of the method: judgment is staged, distributed, and exposed as a problem of jurisdiction rather than assumed as a neutral legal procedure. The web form and the musical form are therefore aligned. Both treat constitutional theory as something that must be staged for readers and audiences who are asked not only to follow an argument but to occupy a position within it.

  • Historical-rhetorical analysis of Federalist and Anti-Federalist texts, with attention to voice, seriality, circulation, and scenes of public judgment.
  • Narrative modeling of how constitutional discourse makes institutions imaginable and assigns limits to legitimate authority.
  • Performance analysis of the Supreme Court as a symbolic stage on which claims of competence, injury, and restraint are enacted.
  • Selective computational comparison where it sharpens, rather than substitutes for, interpretive argument.
  • Native web composition in which site architecture, modular sequencing, and public interface function as part of the dissertation's scholarly method.
  • Dramatic construction of constitutional thought experiments that place rival traditions of American political thought before an audience acting as judge.

Contribution

A contribution to political theory, constitutional thought, rhetoric, and performance studies.

The dissertation contributes to political theory by offering an account of church-state separation as political humility and jurisdictional restraint rather than merely a doctrine of institutional differentiation. It argues that the state's proper limits come into view most clearly when civil authority is asked to adjudicate sacred offense, theological injury, or metaphysical promise.

It contributes to constitutional thought by showing that questions of jurisdiction are inseparable from scenes of public persuasion. Founding-era arguments did not only debate institutions; they staged the intelligibility, plausibility, and emotional credibility of those institutions for a reading public. That archive therefore matters not simply as background to later law, but as the scene in which constitutional authority was first narratively and rhetorically composed.

It contributes to rhetoric and literary-historical analysis by treating constitutional discourse as performed argument: a field of voice, pacing, readership, and serial circulation in which public judgment is organized rather than merely reported. It contributes to performance studies by using Gorham and Brecht to theorize adjudication itself as theatrical judgment and by showing how musical form can operate as a serious method for clarifying the limits of politics.

It also contributes to digital scholarly form by treating medium as part of constitutional argument rather than as a detachable means of delivery. The dissertation is published natively on the web, through self-managed Linode infrastructure, because its architecture and public interface are integral to its claims about staging, legibility, circulation, and judgment. In that sense, the project offers a rigorous account of how medium conditions political argument without collapsing into a generic defense of digital publication.

More broadly, the project offers a way to think about the state's jurisdictional limits without reducing that inquiry either to abstract jurisprudence or to confessional theology. It places constitutional theory, rhetorical form, and staged judgment in the same analytic frame, while also showing that public-facing theory can retain scholarly seriousness when its medium is treated as part of its method.

Publication

Native web publication as the dissertation's primary scholarly form.

This dissertation is published natively on the web. The site itself is the dissertation, not a secondary interface for a more authoritative object stored elsewhere. Its sections, links, pacing, and modular structure constitute the work's principal scholarly form.

Linode is the publication infrastructure for that form. Self-managed infrastructure is not incidental here; it is the material mechanism by which the dissertation appears, circulates, and remains available as a coherent research object. Because the project is concerned with constitutional staging, symbolic mediation, and public judgment, the conditions of publication belong to the argument rather than standing outside it.

Secondary adaptations may still follow. Portions of the project may later be revised into articles, talks, audio work, or classroom modules. Those would be derivative forms, not the source of the dissertation's legitimacy. The primary publication is this native web environment, which joins scholarly argument to the medium through which that argument is staged and judged.

Dissertation Path

Prospective chapter structure.

The dissertation is structured to move from founding-era constitutional discourse into staged constitutional thought experiments about the state's relation to the soul. The trajectory is cumulative rather than split between two unrelated projects.

  • Chapter 1: constitutional discourse, circulation, and public judgment in the ratification era; an account of how institutions became imaginable through staged argument.
  • Chapter 2: Federalist and Anti-Federalist scenes of scale, authority, fear, and legitimacy, with emphasis on narration, readership, and the public formation of constitutional trust.
  • Chapter 3: from founding-era constitutional legibility to jurisdictional restraint; church-state separation, conscience, and the problem of what civil power cannot rightly govern.
  • Chapter 4: Federalists: The Musical, Case 1; sacred offense, blasphemy, and the question of whether the state can restrain expression without certifying sacrilege.
  • Chapter 5: Federalists: The Musical, Case 2; metaphysical contract, rapture insurance, and the question of whether the state can enforce promises that depend on transcendence.
  • Conclusion: the constitutional state as a bounded performer of public judgment, with implications for political theory, legal theater, and the study of spiritual jurisdiction.

Research Plan

A feasible path from prospectus to chapter draft.

Near-term work is organized around three linked tasks: sharpen the theoretical account of jurisdictional restraint, consolidate the founding-era archive that grounds the argument, and draft one chapter that demonstrates how staged constitutional judgment links those materials to the later musical thought experiments.

  • Stage 1: stabilize the founding-era corpus and document which materials are central to the argument about public judgment, mediation, and constitutional legibility.
  • Stage 2: expand the theoretical bibliography around Arendt, Brecht, church-state separation, law and religion, and legal performance.
  • Stage 3: draft the chapter that bridges ratification-era constitutional discourse to the question of spiritual jurisdiction and restraint.
  • Stage 4: develop the two staged Supreme Court cases as analytic chapters rather than appendices, with special attention to how audience judgment functions methodologically.

Revision Note

Major conceptual changes made.

This revision preserves the earlier emphasis on staging, public judgment, rhetorical form, print circulation, and founding-era constitutional discourse, but it no longer treats those as the project's sole end. The prospectus is now organized around the question of the state's limits in spiritual matters. Separation of church and state is framed as jurisdictional restraint rather than mere institutional arrangement, and the founding archive is repositioned as the constitutional groundwork for that claim.

The project also now integrates Hannah Arendt, Eric Gorham, and Bertolt Brecht with distinct analytic functions, and it presents Federalists: The Musical as a serious methodological extension in which fictional Supreme Court cases test whether the state can adjudicate sacred offense or metaphysical promise without exceeding its lawful competence.
