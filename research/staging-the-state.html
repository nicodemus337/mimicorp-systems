Research Project

Staging the State

Prospectus: constitutional argument, public persuasion, and narrative form in the founding-era print sphere.

This project studies how competing constitutional visions were staged for public judgment in the late eighteenth century, with particular attention to how argument, voice, and rhetorical framing operate across the Federalist Papers and selected Anti-Federalist texts. I read these writings not simply as containers of doctrine, but as scenes in which the state had to be narrated before it could be secured.

At stake is how constitutional discourse made institutions imaginable, emotionally legible, and economically persuasive for a reading public that was being asked not simply to interpret a new state, but to inhabit it.

Project Rationale

Why this project matters now.

Scholarship on the founding period has long treated constitutional debate as a problem of doctrine, institutional design, or political theory. I argue that it was also a problem of form. Constitutional order had to be rendered as a scene: danger had to be paced, authority had to be voiced, and the reader had to be positioned inside an unfolding account of what union would secure or destroy.

The intervention therefore sits at the intersection of political theory, rhetoric, and literary-historical analysis. The question is not only what competing authors thought the state should be, but how they scripted consent, fear, legitimacy, and economic order for public uptake.

It also answers a methodological problem that still matters: how to keep institutional history connected to mediation, circulation, and affect. By treating persuasion as form rather than ornament, the project insists that constitutional struggle unfolded through scenes of readership, publicity, and anticipated judgment that made the Union imaginable before it became durable.

Research Question

How was “the state” made legible as a narrative object rather than only a legal one?

The central question is how constitutional authority was narrated, dramatized, and distributed through public writing. Rather than treating founding-era texts as static containers of doctrine, I read them as staged interventions that position readers, anticipate conflict, and rehearse competing understandings of political order.

  • How do Federalist and Anti-Federalist writers construct scenes of danger, scale, and institutional trust?
  • How do pseudonymity, serial publication, and print circulation shape claims to authority and public judgment?
  • How are economic concepts such as credit, commerce, taxation, and consolidation narrated as lived political relations?
  • What kinds of reader are imagined, instructed, flattered, or disciplined by constitutional prose?

Literature Review

Scholarship on ratification, rhetoric, and public formation.

Foundational work on the Revolution and founding-era political thought by Bernard Bailyn, Gordon S. Wood, and Jack N. Rakove establishes the intellectual and constitutional stakes of the ratification era, while scholarship by Saul Cornell, Joyce Appleby, and John Phillip Reid clarifies the relation between republicanism, dissent, representation, and political economy. My project builds on that scholarship but shifts analytical emphasis from doctrine alone to the forms through which doctrine became publicly legible.

Work on Anti-Federalist thought, especially Herbert J. Storing's account of Anti-Federalist political thought, has shown that the debate over the Constitution cannot be reduced to mere resistance or backward-looking localism. I extend that insight by reading Federalist and Anti-Federalist writing as rival modes of staging scale, legitimacy, and institutional feeling for readers asked to imagine the consequences of union.

A second body of scholarship on print culture and publicity, especially Michael Warner's work on publication and the public sphere, alongside debates opened by Bailyn's account of ideological language, suggests that political argument in the eighteenth century operated through circulation, seriality, and mediated address. I take that insight seriously enough to claim that constitutional debate was also a contest over political legibility itself.

The project further draws on public-sphere and rhetorical theory, including Nancy Fraser's critique of the public sphere and Eric B. Gorham's performative model of politics, to argue that constitutional discourse should be read as a staged, relational practice. Rather than choosing between intellectual history, literary analysis, and political science, I treat concepts, genres, and publics as mutually produced.

Theoretical Framework

Politics as performance, political economy as enactment.

This project builds on Eric Gorham's concept of the "Theater of Politics", which treats political life as inherently performative, structured through roles, scripts, and staged interaction.

While Gorham focuses on communication and rhetorical performance, I extend that framework into political economy by analyzing how competing economic logics are not only argued but enacted through structured roles and institutional design.

I also push Gorham's performative tradition toward a broader synthesis with ecological data systems and teaching practice in political science. In this formulation, constitutional discourse can be modeled not only as text or argument but as a dynamic environment of actors, institutions, feedback loops, and situated publics whose relations can be studied across rhetorical, historical, and systems-oriented registers.

Public-sphere theory supplies a second frame. In conversation with Jurgen Habermas, Nancy Fraser, and Michael Warner, the dissertation treats "the public" not as a neutral container for debate but as a historically uneven achievement produced through address, exclusion, seriality, and circulation. Founding-era constitutional writing is therefore analyzed as a technology of public formation as much as a record of opinion.

The broader claim is that constitutional discourse does not merely describe political order. It stages relations among institutions, publics, and economic expectations in a form that solicits identification, resistance, and judgment.

Data + Approach

Primary corpus and analytical frame.

The core corpus consists of the Federalist Papers, a selected body of Anti-Federalist texts including the Brutus letters and Centinel I, and related constitutional commentary used to establish rhetorical context. I combine close reading with computational analysis to trace patterns of diction, address, thematic clustering, and argumentative sequencing across competing constitutional publics.

The archive is deliberately bounded at the outset so that comparison remains legible. From that corpus, I ask how rhetorical performance, institutional imagination, and economic language cohere across opposed constitutional positions.

The corpus will expand in later stages to include constitutional convention debates, selected pamphlet literature, and editorial framing where such materials clarify how these texts circulated and were received within a wider argumentative environment.

Method

Computational comparison paired with narrative modeling.

Methodologically, I pair corpus preparation, token-level comparison, thematic grouping, and rhetorical pattern detection with narrative modeling attentive to scene, speaker position, implied audience, and temporal framing. Computational methods help surface recurrent structures and lexical contrasts; narrative analysis asks how those structures organize political imagination, legitimacy, and fear.

My working hypothesis is that debates over constitutional order are inseparable from performed economic assumptions. Scale, representation, consolidation, credit, and administrative authority appear here not simply as concepts but as enacted relations among speakers, institutions, and publics.

What matters methodologically is not quantification for its own sake, but a way of making formal patterns available for interpretation. Computational comparison helps establish recurrent structures across the archive, while narrative modeling clarifies how those structures stage claims about governance, authority, and economic order.

For that reason, the project is compatible with ecological data systems approaches that map interdependence rather than isolate single variables. Its analytical design can support data structures attentive to circulation, relation, and adaptation, making the project legible not only to literary and rhetorical inquiry but also to political science methods interested in institutions as evolving, environment-shaped systems.

  • Corpus preparation: normalize texts, preserve metadata, and distinguish authorial from editorial framing.
  • Comparative analysis: track lexical clusters around union, liberty, consolidation, faction, credit, taxation, and representation.
  • Narrative modeling: identify speaker posture, implied audience, temporal horizon, and scenes of crisis or reassurance.
  • Interpretive synthesis: connect textual patterns to claims about legitimacy, economic order, and constitutional imagination.
  • Pedagogical translation: adapt the archive and its models for political science teaching on institutions, rhetoric, publics, and systems thinking.

Contribution

A contribution to political theory, rhetorical studies, and early American analysis.

The dissertation contributes a reading of founding-era constitutional debate as a field of performed political economy rather than a purely abstract dispute over institutional form. By treating constitutional argument as staged interaction, it reframes familiar texts as narrative technologies of state formation.

It also contributes methodologically by placing computational comparison in service of interpretive argument rather than substituting quantification for reading. I want the result to remain legible to publication venues in political theory, rhetorical criticism, and interdisciplinary humanities research without surrendering formal ambition.

More specifically, the dissertation aims to show that constitutional language helped produce the very scale of politics it purported only to describe. That claim matters for early American studies because it reframes state formation as a mediated and aesthetic process, and for rhetorical studies because it treats institutional design as inseparable from narrative form.

It also opens a practical bridge to teaching in political science. Because the project treats institutions as staged, relational, and environmentally embedded, its archive and methods can be used to teach constitutional development through simulation, comparative rhetorical analysis, and ecological models of governance rather than through doctrine alone. In that sense, the dissertation extends Gorham's account of political performance toward a natural synthesis with data-rich, interdisciplinary pedagogy.

Dissertation Path

Prospective chapter structure.

The dissertation is structured to move from conceptual framing into corpus design, comparative analysis of Federalist and Anti-Federalist staging, and a concluding account of constitutional discourse as performed political economy.

  • Chapter 1: theoretical framework and literature review, situating the project among constitutional history, rhetorical theory, and public-sphere studies.
  • Chapter 2: corpus, archive, and method, explaining source selection, textual preparation, computational procedure, and interpretive limits.
  • Chapter 3: Federalist scenes of legitimacy, scale, and authority, with attention to union, multiplicity, and the narration of stabilizing distance.
  • Chapter 4: Anti-Federalist scenes of consolidation, fear, and refusal, emphasizing immediacy, threatened liberty, and the drama of remote power.
  • Chapter 5: political economy, narration, and the staging of institutional life, tracing how credit, commerce, and administration become constitutional affects.
  • Conclusion: constitutional discourse as public performance, with implications for democratic theory, media history, and future archival expansion.

Publication Path

Article and public scholarship trajectory.

The project is designed to yield both dissertation chapters and article-length interventions. Individual segments could become essays on founding-era performance, constitutional narration, or the relation between rhetorical form and political economy.

It is also suited to a parallel public-facing track in which selected findings are translated into essayistic or audio form without collapsing the scholarly argument into general-interest summary.

Research Plan

A feasible path from prospectus to chapter draft.

Near-term work is organized around three deliverables: a stabilized pilot corpus, a literature review that clarifies the dissertation's intervention, and one article-scale chapter draft focused on the contrast between Federalist and Anti-Federalist constructions of political scale. That sequence keeps conceptual framing, archival control, and writing development moving together.

  • Stage 1: finalize the primary corpus and document editorial decisions for transcription, normalization, and metadata.
  • Stage 2: complete the historiographic review across constitutional history, Anti-Federalist scholarship, and public-sphere theory.
  • Stage 3: produce a comparative pilot analysis on scale, representation, and consolidation.
  • Stage 4: convert that pilot into a chapter/article draft that establishes the dissertation's signature method.
