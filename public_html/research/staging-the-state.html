Research Project

Staging the State

Prospectus: constitutional argument, public persuasion, and narrative form in the founding-era print sphere.

This prospectus outlines a study of how competing constitutional visions were staged for public judgment in the late eighteenth century, with particular attention to how argument, voice, and rhetorical framing operate across the Federalist Papers and selected Anti-Federalist texts as a contested field of political narration.

Research Question

How was “the state” made legible as a narrative object rather than only a legal one?

The central question asks how constitutional authority was narrated, dramatized, and distributed through public writing. Rather than treating founding-era texts as static containers of doctrine, the project reads them as staged interventions that position readers, anticipate conflict, and model competing understandings of political order.

Theoretical Framework

Politics as performance, political economy as enactment.

This project builds on Eric Gorham's concept of the "Theater of Politics", which treats political life as inherently performative, structured through roles, scripts, and staged interaction.

While Gorham focuses on communication and rhetorical performance, this project extends that framework into political economy by analyzing how competing economic logics are not only argued but enacted through structured roles and institutional design.

Data + Approach

Primary corpus and analytical frame.

The core corpus consists of the Federalist Papers, a selected body of Anti-Federalist texts including the Brutus letters and Centinel I, and related constitutional commentary used to establish rhetorical context. The project combines close reading with computational analysis in order to trace patterns of diction, address, thematic clustering, and argumentative sequencing across competing constitutional publics.

In prospectus terms, the project proceeds from a bounded textual corpus toward a mixed interpretive method: first establishing a comparative archive, then testing how rhetorical performance, institutional imagination, and economic language cohere across opposed constitutional positions.

Method

Computational comparison paired with narrative modeling.

Methodologically, the project combines corpus preparation, token-level comparison, thematic grouping, and rhetorical pattern detection with narrative modeling attentive to scene, speaker position, implied audience, and temporal framing. Computational methods help surface recurrent structures and lexical contrasts, while narrative analysis asks how those structures organize political imagination, legitimacy, and fear.

The working hypothesis is that debates over constitutional order are inseparable from performed economic assumptions: scale, representation, consolidation, credit, and administrative authority appear not simply as concepts but as enacted relations among speakers, institutions, and publics.

In prospectus form, the method is meant to show both evidentiary procedure and interpretive stakes: computational comparison establishes recurring formal patterns across the archive, while narrative modeling clarifies how those patterns stage claims about governance, authority, and economic order.
